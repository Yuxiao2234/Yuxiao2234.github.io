about

I'm an independent researcher in mechanistic interpretability and AI safety, affiliated with the Beneficial AI Foundation at MIT. My work spans sparse autoencoders and crosscoders, the geometry of learned concepts, parameter-efficient fine-tuning, and information-theoretic accounts of how behaviors transfer between models.

Before this: PhD in Electrical Engineering at Tsinghua University, senior research engineering at ByteDance, a postdoc at the Basque Center for Applied Mathematics (BCAM), and MATS 4.0 & 5.0 under Neel Nanda. The industrial detour is why I care about mechanism: behavioral steering alone did not hold up to systemic risk, and I want tools that do.

research

Research directions

Five threads, one through-line: treat model internals as a physical system with measurable structure, so that claims about safety can rest on mechanism. Each direction is larger than any single program; the projects listed are its current active instances.

I

Feature geometry & interpretability infrastructure

The dictionaries our tools learn have geometric structure — and respecting that structure makes the tools better.

foundation

The geometry of concepts

SAE feature point clouds have structure at every scale — crystals, functional lobes, and global clustering — that can be measured and exploited.

Entropy 2025 · with Tegmark et al.
activeSPAR

Crosscoders with structured priors

Vanilla crosscoders scatter semantically related features. Adding manifold-preserving constraints (SNE, InfoNCE, UMAP) recovers semantic neighborhood structure — +31% NDCG@64 over vanilla — at modest reconstruction cost. Includes an SAE Lens crosscoder extension.

paper in preparation
active

V-SAE: variational sparse autoencoders

Making the SAE prior explicit via variational inference. A prior-mismatch account of feature splitting and absorption, with structured priors (correlated, block-diagonal) as the principled fix.

framework & diagnostic study · LessWrong series
II

Principled fine-tuning

Fine-tuning is how the industry adapts models, and it is mostly a black box. Interpretability can tell us where adaptation acts — and where it should.

activeSPAR

Where should we fine-tune?

Layer placement for LoRA interacts strongly with instruction tuning: on instruct models, late-layer LoRA is Pareto-better (equal task accuracy, 53.4% vs 28.8% MMLU retention on Qwen-1.5B-IT), while base models suffer catastrophic forgetting under all-layer adaptation. SAE feature geography varies by architecture — a diagnostic, not a universal recipe.

paper in preparation
activeSPAR

Steering ↔ weight updates

A rank-1 LoRA update on a linear layer acts as activation steering with an input-dependent gate. We study when reward-trained adapters recover prompt-derived in-context vectors, and how the correspondence degrades through non-linearities.

in progress
III

Hidden channels & data-borne bias

Models can inherit behavioral biases from semantically neutral training data. What does the channel carry, at what cost, and can we detect it?

activeSPAR

The geometry of subliminal learning

The mean-activation difference between biased and control number datasets defines a direction that causally induces the hidden preference — steering succeeds when fine-tuning succeeds, making steering vectors a first-order approximation to subliminal transfer.

poster · SPAR Spring 2026
active

Subliminal learning as a communication channel

An information-theoretic framework bounding how much behavioral information can pass through neutral-looking data — encoding cost, channel capacity, and alignment between sender and receiver.

theory-first preprint in preparation
proposed · ET

Bias transfer × concept hierarchy

Do concepts from the same category show similar transfer patterns? Connecting hidden-channel learning to the hierarchical geometry of concepts, via crosscoders across model families.

Effective Thesis direction
IV

Learning between learners

AI systems learn from humans whose beliefs are themselves changing — and changing in systematically biased ways. When is safe assistance possible?

activeMARS

Learning from a biased human learner

Two coupled workstreams: identifiability theory (when can an observer distinguish bias from preference, and when is intervention necessary, not just helpful?) and empirical characterization of real belief change in human–AI interaction — including how faithfully simulated humans stand in for real ones. Belief entrenchment, via the Martingale Score, is the common currency between theory and measurement.

with the Meno LfL agenda · in progress
V

Interpretability for safety-critical deployment

Mechanistic understanding should pay rent: better control in high-stakes settings, and better engineering under real constraints.

activeSPAR

Test-time steering for safety-critical reasoning

Test-time generation of in-context vectors, with SAE features as interpretable targets for safety-aware reward design — evaluated on astrodynamics reasoning (APBench), with Prof. Di Wu.

in progress
proposed · ET

Principled KV-cache compression

An interpretability-first approach to compressing LLM conversation memory — head-specific feature selection with information-theoretic scoring — that scored top in a competitive sparse-attention challenge.

Effective Thesis direction · LessWrong post
proposed · ET

Auditing safety-critical decoders

Interpretability as verification where failure is unacceptable: auditing LLM safety filters, or verifying what brain–computer interface decoders actually compute.

MARS — Learning from a Learner

Summer–Fall 2026

Co-mentored with Tianyi Alex Qiu and Zhonghao He (Meno), as part of MARS V. One unified project — Learning from a Biased Human Learner — with two coupled workstreams: a theory track on identifiability and active assistance, and an empirical track on human-model fidelity and belief change in real human–AI conversations. Kick-off July 2026 (Oxford), outputs targeted for the fall. This program is my deliberate bridge from single-model interpretability into multi-agent safety.
